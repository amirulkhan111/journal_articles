# Sources — Trauma, Memory, and the Unspeakable in Toni Morrison's *Beloved*

## Krumholz, Linda. "The Ghosts of Slavery: Historical Recovery in Toni Morrison's *Beloved*." *African American Review*, vol. 26, no. 3, 1992, pp. 395–408.

- **Verbatim bibliographic record:** Krumholz, Linda. "The Ghosts of Slavery: Historical Recovery in Toni Morrison's *Beloved*." *African American Review*, vol. 26, no. 3, Fiction Issue, Autumn 1992, pp. 395–408. Published by Indiana State University / St. Louis University (via JSTOR, stable URL jstor.org/stable/3041912).
- **Access:** Full-text PDF (JSTOR-digitized scan with OCR text layer) retrieved at <https://bpb-us-w2.wpmucdn.com/blogs.cofc.edu/dist/e/748/files/2015/02/Krumholz-x7gfm8.pdf>; text extracted and page-mapped locally with `pdftotext` to confirm quotations against printed page numbers.
- **Relevant claims / verbatim quotations used:**
  - "In *Beloved* Morrison, like Du Bois in *Souls*, negotiates the legacy of slavery as a national trauma, and as an intensely personal trauma as well." (pp. 395–96)
  - "The central ritual of healing — Sethe's 'rememory' of and confrontation with her past — and the reader's ritual of healing correspond to the three sections of the novel." (p. 396)
  - Quoting the novel itself (Sethe's speech to Denver): "If a house burns down, it's gone, but the place — the picture of it — stays, and not just in my rememory, but out there, in the world. … Right in the place where it happened." (qtd. in Krumholz, p. 395, citing Morrison 36)
  - Quoting the novel's closing refrain: "This is not a story to pass on." (qtd. in Krumholz, p. 407, citing Morrison 275) — Krumholz glosses this as "intensely ironic," since the line denies passing the story on even as the novel itself does exactly that.
  - Krumholz's own Works Cited entry confirms the edition she cites throughout: "Morrison, Toni. *Beloved*. New York: Knopf, 1987."
- **Notes:** Foundational early (1992) reading of *Beloved* as a historical-recovery/healing narrative, read alongside Du Bois. Because I have not independently checked pagination against my own copy of the 1987 Knopf edition, the two Morrison quotations above are cited as "qtd. in Krumholz," not as direct (Morrison ##) citations.

## Koolish, Lynda. "'To Be Loved and Cry Shame': A Psychological Reading of Toni Morrison's *Beloved*." *MELUS*, vol. 26, no. 4, 2001, pp. 169–195.

- **Verbatim bibliographic record:** Koolish, Lynda. "'To Be Loved and Cry Shame': A Psychological Reading of Toni Morrison's *Beloved*." *MELUS*, vol. 26, no. 4, African American Literature issue, Winter 2001, pp. 169–195. Published by the Society for the Study of the Multi-Ethnic Literature of the United States (via JSTOR, stable URL jstor.org/stable/3185546).
- **Access:** Full-text PDF (JSTOR-digitized scan with OCR text layer) retrieved at <https://chsenglishap4.weebly.com/uploads/2/2/5/7/2257880/to_be_loved_and_cry_shame_a_psychological_reading.pdf>; text extracted and page-mapped locally with `pdftotext`.
- **Relevant claims / verbatim quotations used:**
  - "The struggle for psychic wholeness is a continuous one in Toni Morrison's *Beloved*, a novel situated in slavery and its aftermath." (p. 169)
  - "It is a process which requires access to painful memories; the characters in the novel reintegrate, achieve 'the join' … when they no longer refuse the deepest knowledge of the meanings of their individual and historical pasts." (p. 169)
- **Notes:** Psychological/psychoanalytic reading (dissociation, denial) of the novel's characters as a group, not just Sethe. Author affiliation given on the article itself: San Diego State University.

## Lakshmi K, Srividya, et al. "Fragmented Voices and Sonic Resistance: Trauma, Memory, and the Politics of Healing in Toni Morrison's *Beloved*." *Theory and Practice in Language Studies*, vol. 15, no. 12, 2025, pp. 3910–3919.

- **Verbatim bibliographic record:** Lakshmi K, Srividya, Priyadarshini M.C, Karthik Kumar S, and K. Gobalakrishnan. "Fragmented Voices and Sonic Resistance: Trauma, Memory, and the Politics of Healing in Toni Morrison's *Beloved*." *Theory and Practice in Language Studies*, vol. 15, no. 12, December 2025, pp. 3910–3919. DOI: 10.17507/tpls.1512.10.
- **Access:** Open-access journal; full-text PDF retrieved at <https://tpls.academypublication.com/index.php/tpls/article/download/11357/9430/37221>; text extracted and page-mapped locally with `pdftotext` (page range 3910–3919 confirmed in the PDF's own running header).
- **Relevant claims / verbatim quotations used:**
  - "Morrison's concept of 'rememory' captures how painful experiences resurface repeatedly, affecting both individuals and communities." (p. 3910)
  - "The recognisable rhythm that emphasises the growth of trauma inside Sethe's home is established by the opening lines of each book: '124 was spiteful' (Morrison, 1987, p. 13), '124 was loud' (p. 134), and '124 was quiet' (p. 186)." (p. 3916) — the article's own citation of the primary text's three-part structural refrain, given here as "qtd. in Lakshmi K et al." since I have not independently checked these page numbers against my own copy of the 1987 Knopf edition.
- **Notes:** Recent (2025) source, useful to show the topic remains an active area of scholarship. Four authors, affiliated with institutions in India (Vel Tech Rangarajan Dr. Sagunthala R&D Institute of Science and Technology; SRM Institute of Science and Technology; Annamalai University; Malla Reddy Deemed to be University).

- **Verbatim bibliographic record:** Morrison, Toni. "Unspeakable Things Unspoken: The Afro-American Presence in American Literature." *Michigan Quarterly Review*, vol. 28, no. 1, Winter 1989, pp. 1–34. Originally delivered as the University of Michigan Tanner Lecture on Human Values.
- **Access:** Bibliographic details cross-checked via the University of Michigan Quarterly Review digital archive listing (<https://quod.lib.umich.edu/m/mqrarchive/act2080.0028.001>) and independent web search; the archive itself returned HTTP 403 to automated fetch, so full text was **not** read and is **not** quoted in the article — cited only for Morrison's own coinage of the essay's title concept, which Krumholz also references (see Krumholz footnote 4, p. 407).
