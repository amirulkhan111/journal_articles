# Trauma, Memory, and the Unspeakable in Toni Morrison's *Beloved*

## Abstract

Toni Morrison's *Beloved* (1987) has long been read as a novel about how the trauma of slavery survives its own ending — not as settled history but as a force that keeps erupting into the present. This article surveys three strands of that critical conversation: the historical-recovery reading that treats Sethe's "rememory" as a model for collective healing (Krumholz); the psychological reading that treats the novel's characters as dissociated survivors working, unevenly, toward "psychic wholeness" (Koolish); and a recent formal reading that locates trauma's persistence in the novel's fragmented, sonic narrative techniques (Lakshmi et al.). Read together, these accounts converge on a single paradox at the heart of the novel: *Beloved* is built around the claim that its central story is "not a story to pass on," and yet the entire novel is the act of passing it on. The article argues that this paradox — between the unspeakable and the told — is not a flaw in the novel's design but its organizing principle, and that Morrison's own critical vocabulary, developed independently in her essay "Unspeakable Things Unspoken," gives readers a way to name it.

**Keywords:** Toni Morrison, *Beloved*, trauma theory, rememory, African American literature, historical memory

## Introduction

Slavery does not end when *Beloved* opens; it has simply changed shape. The novel's three parts each open with a one-line description of Sethe's house that tracks the changing state of its haunting — "124 was spiteful" (qtd. in Lakshmi K et al. 3916, citing Morrison 13), "124 was loud" (134), "124 was quiet" (186) — a repetition Lakshmi and colleagues read as the formal signature of trauma's persistence across the book (3916). Criticism of the novel since its publication in 1987 has largely agreed that this haunting is not merely a Gothic device but Morrison's way of representing how historical trauma persists in individual and communal memory long after the institutional event that caused it. This article traces how three scholarly accounts — one historicist, one psychological, one formal/narratological — converge on that claim, and argues that the novel's famous closing refrain, "This is not a story to pass on," names the structural paradox that organizes the whole book: a story that insists on its own unspeakability while being, at the same time, told.

## Rememory and Historical Recovery

Linda Krumholz's 1992 essay "The Ghosts of Slavery: Historical Recovery in Toni Morrison's *Beloved*" reads the novel alongside W. E. B. Du Bois's *The Souls of Black Folk*, arguing that "[i]n *Beloved* Morrison, like Du Bois in *Souls*, negotiates the legacy of slavery as a national trauma, and as an intensely personal trauma as well" (Krumholz 395–96). For Krumholz, the novel's central psychological mechanism — what Sethe calls "rememory" — is not simply an individual's recollection but a claim about how the past persists materially in the world, independent of any single consciousness. Sethe explains the concept to Denver in terms that Krumholz quotes directly: "If a house burns down, it's gone, but the place — the picture of it — stays, and not just in my rememory, but out there, in the world. … Right in the place where it happened" (qtd. in Krumholz 395). On this reading, rememory is less a metaphor for trauma than a theory of history: the past remains spatially and communally available to be encountered again, by Sethe or by anyone else who occupies "the place where it happened."

Krumholz extends this individual mechanism into a structural claim about the novel as a whole, arguing that "[t]he central ritual of healing — Sethe's 'rememory' of and confrontation with her past — and the reader's ritual of healing correspond to the three sections of the novel" (396). In other words, the reader is made to undergo a version of the same fragmented, recursive encounter with the past that Sethe undergoes, so that historical recovery becomes as much a property of reading the novel as of the plot it depicts.

That recovery, however, is explicitly framed as a paradox. Krumholz notes that the novel's final line about Beloved — "This is not a story to pass on" (qtd. in Krumholz 407) — is "intensely ironic," since the novel that ends on this refusal is itself the act of passing the story on (407). This tension between the demand for silence and the fact of narration is not incidental; it is, as the next two sections show, precisely where more recent criticism has continued to locate the novel's power.

## Dissociation and the Struggle for Psychic Wholeness

Where Krumholz reads *Beloved* historically, Lynda Koolish's 2001 MELUS essay "'To Be Loved and Cry Shame': A Psychological Reading of Toni Morrison's *Beloved*" reads the novel through the lens of trauma's effects on consciousness itself. Koolish opens by framing the novel's characters collectively, not just Sethe, as caught in an unresolved process: "The struggle for psychic wholeness is a continuous one in Toni Morrison's *Beloved*, a novel situated in slavery and its aftermath" (169). Crucially, Koolish insists that this struggle "requires access to painful memories," and that the novel's characters only "reintegrate, achieve 'the join' … when they no longer refuse the deepest knowledge of the meanings of their individual and historical pasts" (169).

This account reframes what Krumholz calls historical recovery as a psychological cost: healing is not automatic or safe, but requires characters to stop protecting themselves through dissociation and denial. Read alongside Krumholz, Koolish's essay suggests that the "unspeakable" quality of Sethe's story is not only a matter of social taboo — the horror of infanticide — but a matter of psychic defense: some things go unspoken because speaking them is, for the traumatized subject, nearly unsurvivable.

## Fragmentation, Sound, and the Persistence of Trauma

A more recent essay, Srividya Lakshmi K. and colleagues' "Fragmented Voices and Sonic Resistance: Trauma, Memory, and the Politics of Healing in Toni Morrison's *Beloved*" (2025), relocates this same paradox from theme to form. The authors argue that Morrison's narrative technique — its fragmentation, repetition, and use of sound — is itself a representation of how trauma operates on memory, noting that "Morrison's concept of 'rememory' captures how painful experiences resurface repeatedly, affecting both individuals and communities" (Lakshmi K et al. 3910). They locate this technique concretely in the three-part refrain introduced above, arguing that "[t]he recognisable rhythm that emphasises the growth of trauma inside Sethe's home is established by the opening lines of each book" (3916) — a formal device that makes the house itself, rather than any one character's testimony, the register on which trauma's rise and ebb are tracked. This formal reading is consistent with, and updates, the historical and psychological accounts above: if rememory is a claim about how the past persists in the world (Krumholz) and healing requires confronting it psychologically (Koolish), then the novel's fractured, nonlinear structure is the technical means by which Morrison forces the reader to experience that persistence directly, rather than simply being told about it.

That three essays published across more than three decades — 1992, 2001, and 2025 — converge on the same structural paradox suggests it is not an incidental feature of one critical school's vocabulary but a genuine property of the text.

## The Unspeakable, Named

Morrison herself gave later critics a term for this paradox in her 1989 essay "Unspeakable Things Unspoken: The Afro-American Presence in American Literature," delivered as the University of Michigan's Tanner Lecture on Human Values (Morrison, "Unspeakable Things Unspoken"). Krumholz's own notes draw on this essay to gloss how haunting functions in Morrison's fiction (Krumholz 407), which suggests that Morrison's critical and fictional projects were developed in tandem: the novelist who wrote a story that insists it is "not a story to pass on" is the same essayist who theorized, elsewhere and in her own critical voice, what it means for American literature to carry an "unspeakable" presence that nonetheless structures everything around it.

## Conclusion

Read together, Krumholz's historicist account, Koolish's psychological account, and Lakshmi and colleagues' formal account describe the same underlying structure from three different angles: *Beloved* is organized around a story that cannot be told and must be told at once. Sethe's "rememory" (qtd. in Krumholz 395) makes the past a standing feature of the world rather than a closed event; Koolish's account of "psychic wholeness" (169) shows why confronting that past is so costly for the novel's characters; and Lakshmi and colleagues show how Morrison encodes that cost formally, in fragmentation and repetition, rather than only thematically. The paradox named in the novel's final refrain — "not a story to pass on" and yet passed on regardless (qtd. in Krumholz 407) — is therefore best understood not as a contradiction to be resolved but as *Beloved*'s central technical and ethical achievement: a demonstration that some histories survive only by being told in a form that keeps insisting they cannot be.
