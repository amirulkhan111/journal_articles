# Sources — Oral and Textual Authority Across the Regional Ramkathas

## Ramanujan, A. K. "Three Hundred Ramayanas: Five Examples and Three Thoughts on Translation." *The Collected Essays of A. K. Ramanujan*, edited by Vinay Dharwadker, Oxford University Press, 1999, pp. 131–160.

- **Verbatim bibliographic record:** Ramanujan, A. K. "Three Hundred Ramayanas: Five Examples and Three Thoughts on Translation." *The Collected Essays of A. K. Ramanujan*, edited by Vinay Dharwadker, Oxford University Press, 1999, pp. 131–160. (Essay originally written 1987, first published in Paula Richman, editor, *Many Ramayanas: The Diversity of a Narrative Tradition in South Asia*, University of California Press, 1991 — **I have not read that 1991 printing**, only the 1999 *Collected Essays* reprint below, so all page citations refer to the 1999 edition's own pagination, not the 1991 original's.)
- **Access:** Full-text PDF retrieved at <https://www.trans-techresearch.net/wp-content/uploads/2015/05/three-hundred-Ramayanas-A-K-Ramanujan.pdf>; extracted locally with `pdftotext`. **OCR quality is poor and uneven** — diacritics (ā, ī, ṇ, ś, etc.) are frequently misread as stray capital letters embedded mid-word (e.g. "Vālmīki" → "VglmIki" / "Vglmiki"; "Rāma" → "RBma" / "RHma"), and spacing is sometimes dropped between words. Every quotation below was checked against this raw extraction and diacritic/spacing artifacts were silently normalized to standard romanization **only** where the underlying word was unambiguous (matching well-documented, frequently-cited lines); no wording, word order, or punctuation was otherwise altered. Page numbers were confirmed against explicit page-footer text ("132 The Collected Essays of A.K. Ramanujan" / "Three Hundred Ramayanas / 135" etc.) visible in the OCR at each cited location. I was **not** able to independently confirm the essay's exact starting page (131) or ending page (160) against a footer marker — 132 is the earliest page-numbered marker I directly observed, and 160 the latest before the next essay ("Repetition in the Mahābhārata") begins in the same PDF; the "131–160" range is the standard citation for this essay in this edition per secondary sources, not something I independently verified end-to-end.
- **Relevant claims / verbatim quotations used:**
  - Frame story (opens the essay): the King of Spirits shows Hanuman a platter of identical rings and says, "There have been as many Rāmas as there are rings on this platter. When you return to earth, you will not find Rāma. This incarnation of Rāma is now over." (p. 132)
  - "I have come to prefer the word *tellings* to the usual terms *versions* or *variants* because the latter terms can and typically do imply that there is an invariant, an original or Ur-text — usually Vālmīki's Sanskrit *Rāmāyaṇa*, the earliest and most prestigious of them all. But as we shall see, it is not always Valmiki's narrative that is carried from one language to another." (p. 134)
  - "The tradition itself distinguishes between the Rāma story (*rāmakathā*) and texts composed by a specific person — Vālmīki, Kampan, or Kṛttivāsa, for example." (p. 134)
  - "In Vālmīki, Rāma's character is not that of a god but of a god-man who has to live within the limits of a human form with all its vicissitudes... Be that as it may, in Kampan he is clearly a god." (p. 142)
  - "Thus the Ahalyā episode is essentially the same, but the weave, the texture, the colours are very different. Part of the aesthetic pleasure in the later poet's telling derives from its artistic use of its predecessor's work, from ringing changes on it. To some extent all later Rāmāyaṇas play on the knowledge of previous tellings: they are meta-Rāmāyaṇas." (p. 142)
  - On the Jain tellings: "When we enter the world of Jain tellings, the Rāma story no longer carries Hindu values." (p. 144)
  - Quoting Santosh Desai on the story's geographic transmission routes: "By land, the northern route took the story from the Punjab and Kashmir into China, Tibet, and East Turkestan; by sea, the southern route carried the story from Gujarat and South India into Java, Sumatra, and Malaya; and again by land, the eastern route delivered the story from Bengal into Burma, Thailand, and Laos. Vietnam and Cambodia obtained their stories partly from Java and partly from India via the eastern route" (qtd. in Ramanujan 144, citing Desai 1970, 5) — I have not independently read Desai's 1970 study; this is Ramanujan's own citation, reproduced as "qtd. in Ramanujan."
  - "In fact, to our modern eyes, this Rāvana is a tragic figure; we are moved to admiration and pity for Ravana when the Jains tell the story." (p. 145)
  - "In another tradition of the Jain Ramayanas, Sita is his daughter, although he does not know it: the dice of tragedy are loaded against him further by this oedipal situation." (p. 145)
  - Closing frame story ("What Happens When You Listen"), introducing the essay's second Hanuman's-ring tale: "This essay opened with a folktale about the many Rāmāyaṇas. Before we close, it may be appropriate to tell another tale about Hanumān and Rāma's ring. But this story is about the power of the *Rāmāyaṇa*, about what happens when you really listen to this potent story." (p. 160)
- **Notes:** This is the foundational text for the entire "many tellings, not one authoritative version" argument that organizes the article. Ramanujan explicitly frames the whole essay between two oral folktales about Hanuman's ring — the theoretical argument about textual plurality is itself bracketed by orality, which is directly relevant to this article's angle.

- **Verbatim bibliographic record:** Parthasarathy, Indira. "The Rama Story as Embellished by Kamban." *Delhi Journal of Comparative Studies*, vol. 1, 2021–22, delhicomparatists.org/2021/08/the-rama-story-as-embellished-by-kamban/.

Editorial/peer-review status of this specific journal is not confirmed beyond the site showing an "Editorial Board" page — it may be journal-affiliated web publication rather than a formally peer-reviewed print article; flagged as an open question, not concealed.
- **Relevant claims / verbatim quotations used:**
  - "Kamban did not translate the Valmiki *Ramayana* but transcreated it with masterly and subtle structural changes to suit his own literary views and concepts, without offending the sequential order of narration in the original."
  - "Kamban was a conscious literary artist, who had it clear in his mind that what he proposed to write was a literary piece and not a religious work — that was how Valmiki's *Ramayana* came to be known during the period of Kamban, though Valmiki might not have intended it to be so."
  - "Kumbhakarna was an uncouth figure, a man-mountain, a glutton, and a demon in Valmiki's *Ramayana*."
- **Notes:** Author is the well-known Tamil writer and Sahitya Akademi–recognized scholar Indira Parthasarathy (this identification is based on the byline and general knowledge, not independently cross-checked against a separate biographical source — flagged as a minor unverified detail).
